What is antimicrobial resistance?

Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) occurs when bacteria, viruses, and other organisms develop a way to prevent antimicrobial drugs (like antibiotics) from effectively killing them or controlling their growth.81, 82 As a result of drug resistance, antibiotics and other antimicrobial medicines become ineffective, and pathogens do not respond to the drugs designed to kill them. This means that infections that used to be treatable can become increasingly difficult or impossible to treat.81, 83

The number of effective antibiotics is steadily decreasing, and to such an extent that in future there may be no antibiotics left that can successfully combat infections.79 The potential scope of the problem is so great that the WHO says that without urgent action, the world is heading for a post-antibiotic era, in which common infections and minor injuries can once again kill.82, 84, 85

Unfortunately, no new classes of antibiotics have been discovered since the 1980s. A class means a type of antibiotic that works in a certain way, for example attacking a specific germ function (this is called its mode of action).6, 34, 80, 86 However, due to the prevalence of AMR, the search for new classes of antibacterial agents has now spread to the likes of metal ions, nanoparticles, and metal compounds.87, 88

The future of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) treatment

Evans and Kavanagh34 published a paper in which they investigated the use of metals as antibacterial agents. They say that metals have a history of antibacterial uses, but with the discovery of antibiotics became overlooked and effectively discarded and ignored. According to them, metal-based antibacterial compounds have novel modes of action that may provide an advantage in the treatment of bacterial infections with resistance to conventional antibiotics.

An ideal novel antibacterial compound must be able to combat both the acquired and the intrinsic resistance mechanisms of bacteria.33 Metal-based antibacterial compounds have been proposed as a candidate for the development of a novel class of antibiotics for the effective treatment of antibiotic-resistant bacteria.34, 35
